Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine (DICOM) Last updated 11/18/2013

DICOM files are the means by which the medical community communicates medical imaging data. It creates a standard to transmit medical images in a complex and dynamic environment of cutting-edge, innovative technologies. The standard is maintained by the Medical Imaging and Technology Alliance (MITA), which is a division of the National Electrical Manufacturers Association (NEMA). The DICOM standard is freely available here.

A Brief History

Computed Tomography (CT) emerged as a medical diagnostic utility in the 1970s and with it other influential modalities. The American College of Radiology (ACR) and NEMA formed a jount committee in 1983 to create a standard that adhered to certain principles that are necessary for succint transmission of medical images.

  • Promote communication of digital image information, regardless of device manufacturer
  • Facilitate the development and expansion of picture archiving and communication systems (PACS) that can also interface with other systems of hospital information
  • Allow the creation of diagnostic information data bases that can be interrogated by a wide variety of devices distributed geographically.

A Functional Overview

The DICOM standard was designed to set minimum software commands, consistent data organization, and interfacing with medical imaging hardware. The principles that were necessary for a medical imaging standard are the following:

  • Ability to interface with computer networks
  • Ability to be functional offline
  • Specifies how data should be communicated between devices, computers, and personel
  • Specifies a multipart document: header (patient, study, series) data and image data
  • Specifies explicit Information Objects for images, waveforms, and printing
  • Specifies strict definitions and relational organization of Information Objects
A DICOM file is broken up into objects that are perfectly relational. A patient can have many medical scans conducted throughout their lifetime, therefore he or she can have multiple studies performed on them. Within any single study, the patient could be asked to change positions, which correspond to series. Within any single series, there are multiple images being captured by the medical imaging hardware.

A Technical Overview

As was mentioned above, a DICOM file contains multiple objects that relate to patient, study, series, and image information. These are defined by the following Unique Identifiers (UID):

  • Patient: Patient ID
  • Study: Study Instance UID
  • Series: Series Instance UID
  • Image: SOP Instance UID
A patient can have multiple studies; a study can have multiple series; a series can have multiple images, linked together relationally by the keys mentioned above. This is how I have organized the relational database (postgreSQL) for this website -- excluding patient data. Before any file is uploaded to our servers, the patient data is anonymized.
